Strategic Framework

Report Card Season = Peak Parent Anxiety + Student Stress. This is our opportunity window.

The Core Insight

Parents don't just need tutoring. They need peace of mind. Students don't just need help passing. They need confidence restoration. The report card is both a trigger point (pain) and a decision point (action). March creates a high-intent window where parents are actively seeking solutions when they see declining or poor performance.

The Customer Journey Map

Three distinct emotional phases requiring tailored messaging and content delivery.

The Progression

AWARENESS
(Pre-Report) → CONSIDERATION
(Report Week) → DECISION
(Post-Report) → ACTION
(Enrollment)
1
Pre-Report Phase
EARLY MARCH • WEEK 1-2

Emotional State:

Anticipation, anxiety, denial, hope

Pain Points:

  • Parents: "I think my child is struggling but I'm not sure. Should I wait for the report card or act now?"
  • Students: "I know I'm behind but maybe I can still pull it off before exams."

Content Goal:

Make the invisible visible. Surface the anxiety. Normalize seeking help before the damage is done.

Marketing Message: "You already know something's not right. Don't wait for the report card to confirm it."
2
Report Card Week
MID MARCH • WEEK 3

Emotional State:

Shock, disappointment, guilt, panic

Pain Points:

  • Parents: "The grades are worse than I thought. What do I do NOW? Is it too late?"
  • Students: "I failed/underperformed. Everyone's disappointed. I feel stupid."

Content Goal:

Meet them in the emotion, validate the feelings, pivot to "what's next." This is NOT the time for "I told you so." It's time for empathy + solution.

Marketing Message: "This report card hurts. But it's not the final word. Here's what you do next."
3
Post-Report Phase
LATE MARCH • WEEK 4

Emotional State:

Motivated, determined, ready to act OR overwhelmed and paralyzed

Pain Points:

  • Parents: "I want to help but I don't know where to start. Tutoring? Which one? How do I know it'll work?"
  • Students: "I want to do better next term but I don't even know where I went wrong."

Content Goal:

Provide the roadmap. Show the transformation. Remove the mystery around "what tutoring actually does." Make enrollment feel like the obvious next step.

Marketing Message: "Next term doesn't have to be a repeat. Here's exactly how MathEtc turns report card pain into progress."

Content Campaign Structure: 6 Videos

Cinematic storytelling via young creators (25-28 yrs old) speaking directly to camera. Studio-shot with dynamic angles, emotionally resonant, authentic.

1
PHASE 1: ANTICIPATION

"That Feeling You're Ignoring"

👥 Target: Parents
"You already know. You're just waiting for the report card to make it official."

Angle: Young creator talking directly to camera about the subtle signs parents miss or ignore — kid suddenly "busy" when you ask about school, grades slowly slipping, excuses piling up. NOT told from a parent character's POV but AS a person who sees it happening in their family/community.

PAIN ADDRESSED Gut feeling that something's wrong but choosing to "wait and see"
CTA SEED "What if you didn't wait? What if you handled it now, while there's still time to turn the term around?"
MATHETC MENTION Subtle. "I've been telling my sister about MathEtc because her son's been acting exactly like this…"
2
PHASE 1: ANTICIPATION

"The Pressure They Won't Tell You About"

👥 Target: Parents (about their kids) + Students
"Your kid says they're fine. They're not fine."

Angle: Creator unpacking the internal pressure students face during report card season — the shame of falling behind, the anxiety of disappointing parents, the isolation of feeling like everyone else "gets it" except them. This builds empathy and urgency.

PAIN ADDRESSED Students are silently drowning under pressure and parents don't realize how bad it is
CTA SEED "If your child is withdrawn, irritable, or avoiding school talk… that's not attitude. That's stress. And stress without support becomes failure."
MATHETC MENTION Moderate. "When my cousin started tutoring with MathEtc, it wasn't just about the grades. It was the first time in months she didn't feel like she was failing alone."
3
PHASE 2: D-DAY

"When the Report Card Is Worse Than You Expected"

👥 Target: Parents
"You open it. You freeze. And then you think: How did I miss this?"

Angle: Creator walking through the emotional aftermath of getting a bad report card — not as the parent, but as someone addressing parents directly. The blame spiral ("Was I too busy? Did I ask enough questions?"), the anger at the child, the anger at the school, the anger at yourself. Then the pivot: "But here's the truth — this report card is just information. What you do with it is what matters."

PAIN ADDRESSED Shock, guilt, disappointment — the emotional gut punch of seeing bad grades
CTA SEED "You have two choices. React emotionally and hope next term is different. Or respond strategically and make next term different."
MATHETC MENTION Strong. "This is where parents I know turn to MathEtc. Not as punishment. As a plan. Because the next 6 weeks decide whether this was a wake-up call or the beginning of a longer slide."
4
PHASE 2: D-DAY

"If You're a Student Watching This..."

👥 Target: Students (but parents will watch too)
"You already know what the report card says. And you already feel terrible about it. So let's skip the guilt trip and talk about what's real."

Angle: Creator speaking directly to the student — validating the feeling of falling behind, the trap of avoiding the subjects you're weak in, the fear of asking for help because it feels like admitting you're stupid. Then the reframe: "You're not dumb. You're stuck. And stuck is fixable."

PAIN ADDRESSED The shame and helplessness of underperforming + the spiral of giving up
CTA SEED "Next term starts in a few weeks. You can go in hoping things magically get better, or you can go in prepared. One of those actually works."
MATHETC MENTION Strong/Authentic. "I know people who were failing Math in Term 1 and passed Term 2 because they got actual help. Not YouTube tutorials. Not cramming. Structured, personalized tutoring with MathEtc."
5
PHASE 3: RECOVERY

"What to Actually Do After a Bad Report Card"

👥 Target: Parents
"You've done the hard conversation. You've processed the disappointment. Now what?"

Angle: Creator laying out the practical, strategic next steps. This is NOT emotional — it's tactical. What parents should be doing this week to set their child up for next term. Break the overwhelm into clear actions: 1) Identify the weakest subjects, 2) Find out why they're struggling, 3) Get external support that's diagnostic, not just "more homework."

PAIN ADDRESSED Wanting to help but not knowing where to start; fear of choosing the wrong solution
CTA SEED "Hoping your child figures it out on their own is not a strategy. Structure is. Support is."
MATHETC MENTION Direct/Specific. "This is exactly what MathEtc does. They don't just tutor. They audit where the breakdown happened, build a custom learning plan, and work with your child consistently until the foundation is solid."
6
PHASE 3: RECOVERY

"Next Term, Different Story"

👥 Target: Students + Parents
"You don't have to repeat this term. You just have to do it differently."

Angle: This is the hope video. The "comeback is real" energy. Creator speaking to both students and parents — acknowledging that change is hard, improvement takes work, but it's absolutely possible if you start now and do it right. Share a real or realistic example: "I know someone who went from 38% in Math to 62% in one term. Not because they became a genius overnight. Because they got the right help and actually put in the work."

PAIN ADDRESSED Fear that nothing will change; lack of belief that improvement is possible
CTA SEED "The next report card is being written right now. Every session, every chapter, every question answered correctly is a step toward a different result. But you have to start."
MATHETC MENTION Full Integration. "MathEtc isn't magic. It's method. It's sitting with someone who knows how to teach, who won't skip over the hard parts, who will make sure you actually understand before moving forward."

📹 Talking Head Format

  • Primary Angle: Medium close-up (MCU) — creator speaks directly to camera, intimate, conversational
  • Secondary Angle: Slightly wider or off-angle (over-shoulder, profile, pulled-back) — adds visual dynamic
  • Camera Movement: Subtle. Maybe a slow push-in during emotional peaks, a slight drift during reflective moments. Nothing gimmicky.

🛋️ Studio Setup (Living Room Corner)

  • Couch or comfy chair
  • Warm, ambient lighting (ring light + practical lamps in the background)
  • Books, plants, small decor — lived-in, relatable, not clinical
  • Consistent across all 6 videos to build visual brand continuity

🎞️ Secondary Clips/B-Roll (All Shot in Studio)

These inserts are atmospheric, NOT literal scenes. They punctuate the monologue, give visual rest, and subtly reinforce the emotional beats.

  • Close-ups of hands holding/opening a report card
  • Textbook pages, notebooks, pens, study materials
  • Phone screen showing messages, notifications, MathEtc website
  • A school bag on the floor
  • A calendar/planner being filled in
  • Empty chair at a desk (anticipation)
  • Two chairs at a table (tutoring session implication)

Email Marketing Campaign Strategy

Parallel email campaign targeting existing database and captured leads from video content. Same emotional journey, different medium.

Email Campaign Structure

Total Emails: 6 (matching the 6-video structure)

Frequency: 2 emails per week (Tuesday & Thursday at 7 PM)

Target Lists:

  • Existing MathEtc customer database (parents of past/current students)
  • Website visitors who downloaded resources
  • Lead magnet captures from quiz/assessment
  • Social media video engagers (retargeted via email)

Email 1: "Is Your Child Quietly Struggling?"

Week 1 • Tuesday Target: All Parents Phase: Anticipation
Subject Line: "That gut feeling you've been ignoring... (it's real)"

Content Angle: Opens with relatable scenario — child avoiding homework conversations, grades slowly slipping, excuses piling up. Validates parent's instinct that something's off. Introduces the idea that waiting for the report card is reactive, not proactive.

CTA: "Take our 2-minute Parent Assessment Quiz" (captures lead, segments by concern level)

Email 2: "What Your Child Won't Tell You"

Week 1 • Thursday Target: All Parents Phase: Anticipation
Subject Line: "Your child says 'I'm fine.' Here's what that really means."

Content Angle: Unpacks the silent stress students carry — shame of falling behind, fear of disappointing parents, isolation. Positions MathEtc as the support system that takes pressure OFF the parent-child relationship by providing external, judgment-free help.

CTA: "Watch: The Pressure They Won't Tell You About" (links to Video 2)

Email 3: "Report Cards Are Coming"

Week 2 • Tuesday Target: Quiz completers + High concern segment Phase: D-Day Prep
Subject Line: "Report cards drop this week. Here's what to do if it's bad news."

Content Angle: Acknowledges the anxiety of waiting. Provides a "what to do if..." framework: If the report is worse than expected, here are the next 3 steps. Positions MathEtc as Step 1 (diagnose the root problem).

CTA: "Book a Free Diagnostic Session (Before Results Day)" — urgency play

Email 4: "After the Report Card Shock"

Week 3 • Tuesday Target: All segments Phase: D-Day
Subject Line: "You opened it. You froze. Now what?"

Content Angle: This email meets parents in the immediate aftermath. Validates the emotional response (guilt, disappointment, anger). Then pivots hard: "This is information, not a verdict. What you do in the next 48 hours determines whether this is a wake-up call or the start of a slide."

CTA: "Schedule Emergency Recovery Session" — time-sensitive offer

Email 5: "The Recovery Roadmap"

Week 3 • Thursday Target: Video viewers + Email openers from Email 4 Phase: Recovery
Subject Line: "You've had the hard conversation. Here's the actual plan."

Content Angle: Tactical, step-by-step breakdown. Not emotional, just practical. "Here's what parents who successfully turned this around did: 1) Identified weakest subjects, 2) Found diagnostic tutoring, 3) Committed to 6-8 weeks of consistency." Case study or testimonial snippet.

CTA: "Get Your Custom Recovery Plan" — link to booking with intake form

Email 6: "Next Term Starts Now"

Week 4 • Tuesday Target: All engaged segments Phase: Final Push
Subject Line: "Your child's next report card is being written right now."

Content Angle: Hope + urgency. Real example of transformation (38% to 62% in one term). "This didn't happen by accident. It happened because of structure, support, and starting NOW. Not next month. Not after another bad test. Now." Final enrollment push with scarcity (limited March slots).

CTA: "Secure Your Spot — March Enrollment Ends Soon"
